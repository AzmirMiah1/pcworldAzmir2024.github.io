The Need for Input Devices

Various types of input devices are one of the components of a computer system where users feed in data and commands into a system. If there were no input devices, the users would be unable to provide the data the computer needs to perform the instructed actions.


Examples of Input Devices

  • Keyboard: Used to type text, issue commands, and input data into applications like word processors and spreadsheets.
  • Mouse: A pointing device used to navigate graphical interfaces, click, drag, and interact with on-screen elements.
  • Touchscreen: Combines input and output functionalities, enabling users to interact directly with what is displayed.
  • Microphone: Captures audio input for tasks like voice recognition, video calls, and multimedia creation.
  • Scanner: Converts physical documents or images into digital formats.

The Need for Output Devices

Output devices enable a user to perceive or read the results of the process that has been done by the computer system. Such devices enable information processed by a computer to be presented in forms that are visible, audible, or in any other way.


Examples of Output Devices

  • Monitor: Displays graphical and textual output, enabling users to see the results of their actions. It’s crucial for tasks ranging from web browsing to video editing.
  • Printer: Produces physical copies of documents, photos, or graphics for professional, educational, or personal use.
  • Speaker: Output audio signals, allowing users to hear music, system sounds, or multimedia content.
  • Headphones: A personal audio output device for immersive sound experiences in gaming, music, or communication.
  • Projector: Projects images or videos onto larger surfaces, ideal for presentations, education, or entertainment.

The Need for Peripheral Hardware

Peripheral hardware comprises all hardware devices that are used externally to enhance the capability of a computer. Such devices can be considered as input/output and storage peripherals, and they are essential for adjusting the computer systems to meet the demands of certain users.


Examples of Peripheral Hardware

  • External Hard Drives: Provide additional space for storing large amounts of data or backing up important files.
  • Webcam: Captures video input for video calls, streaming, or surveillance purposes.
  • External GPU Enhances a computer’s graphics processing capabilities, essential for gaming, 3D modeling, or video rendering.
  • USB Flash Drives Portable storage devices used for transferring files between systems or for temporary data storage.
  • Docking Stations Expand the connectivity of laptops by adding ports for USB devices, Ethernet, or external monitors.

Comparison of Ports: Purpose, Function, and Speed

  • USB type-C connections are common today and take over older standards such as Micro-B because they are effective and can transfer power. LAN inputs are still needed to achieve high-speed and better performance connections. On the other end of the spectrum, ports such as parallel and COM are considered to be outdated in modern personal computing due to their relatively lower speeds, and dynamic and unattractive physical structure. The various port types are however used based on the hardware and the application as mentioned below.

    USB (Universal Serial Bus)

  • Purpose: USB is a multi-functional interface aimed at providing interaction between computers and an extended list of peripherals and devices such as keyboards, mice, printers, drives, and phones. Alternatively, it is capable of supporting both data and power routes.
  • Function: USB ports are an improved way of connecting a device to a computer as they replace other older and slower ports. Current day types such as USB 3.0 or USB type C are compatible with previous device technologies.
  • Speed:
    • USB 2.0: Up to 480 Mbps
    • USB 3.0: Up to 5 Gbps
    • USB 3.1/3.2: Up to 10–20 Gbps
    • USB4: Up to 40 Gbps

    Parallel Port

  • Purpose: Parallel ports were commonly used in transfer of data to peripheral devices such as printers. They enable parallel transmission of several bits of data.
  • Function: Data transmission is also parallel, which means that 8 or more bits are transmitted simultaneously, through several lines of data transfer.
  • Speed:
    • Standard parallel port (SPP): Up to 150 Kbps.
    • Enhanced parallel port (EPP): Up to 2 Mbps.
    • Extended capabilities port (ECP): Up to 2.5 Mbps.

    LAN (Local Area Network) Port

  • Purpose: A LAN port is an Ethernet port that is used to connect devices to a wired network to access internet connection or other resources on the local network.
  • Function: They define a direct, high-speed pathway to a network, thereby guaranteeing communication coherency and data exchange quality.
  • Speed:
    • Fast Ethernet: Up to 100 Mbps.
    • Gigabit Ethernet: Up to 1 Gbps.
    • 10-Gigabit Ethernet: Up to 10 Gbps.

    COM (Serial Port)

  • Purpose: Serial ports are employed for connectivity of the computer and other accessories such as modem, sensors or other traditional industrial operations.
  • Function: Data is transmitted sequentially, one bit at a time, through a single data line.
  • Speed:
    • RS-232 standard: Up to 115.2 Kbps.
    • Advanced versions: Up to 1 Mbps (depending on hardware).
